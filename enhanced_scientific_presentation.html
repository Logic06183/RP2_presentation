Comprehensive Climate-Health Comparison

Figure 2. Multi-dimensional climate comparison between Johannesburg and Abidjan. Top: Daily temperature time series (2020-2024) and seasonal climatology. Middle: Temperature-humidity relationships by season showing distinct climate signatures. Bottom: Heat index categorization and extreme heat day trends revealing divergent heat exposure patterns between subtropical highland and tropical savanna climates.
Climate Zone Characterization
  • Johannesburg: Subtropical highland (1,753m) - moderate temperature, low humidity stress
  • Abidjan: Tropical savanna (76m) - high temperature-humidity combined stress
  • Heat Index Analysis: Abidjan shows 4.8x more extreme caution days than Johannesburg
  • Temporal Trends: Increasing extreme heat frequency in both cities (2020-2024)

Spatial Principal Components Analysis

Figure 3. Geographically weighted principal component analysis revealing spatial heterogeneity in heat-health vulnerability. PC1 (Environmental Exposure): Northern regions show elevated exposure scores. PC2 (Health Vulnerability): Southern townships demonstrate higher health vulnerability concentrations.

Environmental Exposure (PC1)

  • Northern industrial corridors show maximum exposure
  • Correlates with land surface temperature (r=0.73)
  • Urban heat island intensity drives spatial patterns

Health Vulnerability (PC2)

  • Southern townships show concentrated vulnerability
  • Socioeconomic constraints limit adaptive capacity
  • Health infrastructure access patterns

Geographically Weighted Heat Vulnerability Index

Figure 4. Spatially explicit heat vulnerability index (left) and top 10 most vulnerable wards identification (right). Index integrates exposure, sensitivity, and adaptive capacity using geographically weighted approaches, enabling precise spatial targeting for climate adaptation interventions.
Spatial Targeting Results
Top 10 vulnerable wards identified through multi-criteria analysis encompass 890,000 residents (18% of metropolitan population) requiring priority heat-health interventions. Vulnerability concentration enables efficient resource allocation for maximum population health impact.

Local Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA)

Figure 5. LISA cluster analysis demonstrating statistically significant spatial autocorrelation (Global Moran's I p-value: <2e-16). High-high vulnerability clusters (red) concentrated in southern townships, low-low clusters (blue) in northern suburbs, validating geographically explicit analytical approach.

Statistical Validation

  • Global Moran's I: Highly significant spatial clustering (p < 2e-16)
  • Local clusters: 89% of high-vulnerability areas show spatial autocorrelation
  • Robustness: Results consistent across multiple spatial weight matrices

Policy Implications

Intervention Clustering
Spatial clustering enables efficient intervention design through geographic proximity, reducing implementation costs while maximizing coverage of vulnerable populations.

Climate Variables Validation Matrix

Figure 7. Pearson correlation matrix for ERA5 climate variables demonstrating data consistency and inter-variable relationships. High correlations between temperature measures (r=0.67-0.97) and land surface temperature variables (r=0.78-0.97) validate multi-temporal analytical approach for heat exposure assessment.
Data Validation Results
  • Temporal Consistency: High correlations across 1D, 7D, 30D temperature means (r > 0.82)
  • Satellite Validation: ERA5 temperature strongly correlates with MODIS LST (r=0.66-0.70)
  • Spatial Coherence: Land surface temperature shows strong internal consistency (r=0.97)
  • Multi-source Integration: Robust data fusion enables comprehensive climate-health analysis

Executive Policy Dashboard

Figure 8. Executive summary dashboard for public health planning. Key metrics: Johannesburg (16°C average, 25 extreme heat days/year, 2.5M at-risk population, $140M economic impact) vs Abidjan (27°C average, 120 extreme heat days/year, 4.5M at-risk population, $150M economic impact).
Key Policy Recommendations
  • Johannesburg: Implement extreme heat early warning systems for dry heat events >35°C
  • Abidjan: Prioritize humidity-heat stress monitoring and cooling infrastructure
  • Both Cities: Strengthen healthcare capacity during peak heat seasons
  • Investment Priority: Heat-resilient urban planning and green infrastructure
  • Data Systems: Establish real-time heat-health surveillance networks
