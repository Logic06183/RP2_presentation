Spatial Principal Components Analysis

Figure 1. Spatial distribution of PC1 (Environmental Exposure Factors) and PC2 (Health Vulnerability Factors) across Johannesburg metropolitan area. PC1 explains environmental heat exposure gradients, while PC2 captures socio-demographic vulnerability patterns. Blue indicates higher loadings, red indicates lower loadings.
Environmental Exposure (PC1)
Northern regions show elevated environmental exposure scores, correlating with land surface temperature and urban heat island intensity.
Health Vulnerability (PC2)
Southern townships demonstrate higher vulnerability scores, reflecting socioeconomic constraints and limited adaptive capacity.

Geographically Weighted Heat Vulnerability Index

Figure 2. Spatially explicit heat vulnerability index (left) and identification of top 10 most heat-vulnerable wards (right) in Johannesburg. Vulnerability index integrates exposure, sensitivity, and adaptive capacity components using geographically weighted principal component analysis.
Methodological Innovation
  • Geographically Weighted Principal Component Analysis (GW-PCA) for spatial non-stationarity
  • Multi-domain vulnerability assessment incorporating climate, health, and socioeconomic indicators
  • Ward-level analysis enabling targeted intervention strategies
  • Validation through Local Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA) clustering

Local Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA)

Figure 3. LISA cluster analysis revealing significant spatial autocorrelation in heat vulnerability (Global Moran's I p-value: <2e-16). Red clusters indicate high-high vulnerability hotspots, blue clusters show low-low vulnerability areas, with statistically significant spatial clustering at p < 0.05.

Spatial Clustering Results

  • High-High Clusters: Concentrated vulnerability hotspots in southern townships
  • Low-Low Clusters: Northern suburban areas with reduced vulnerability
  • Spatial Autocorrelation: Highly significant (p < 2e-16) indicating robust clustering
  • Policy Implications: Clear geographic targeting for interventions

Statistical Significance

Global Moran's I
p-value < 2e-16 confirms statistically significant spatial clustering, validating the geographically explicit approach to vulnerability assessment.

Research Capacity Summary

Analytical Potential
Two contrasting climate zones with overlapping vulnerable populations provide ideal natural experiment for heat-health vulnerability analysis. Rich clinical biomarker data enables mechanistic understanding of heat exposure pathways.

Scientific Contributions & Future Directions

Methodological Innovation
First application of geographically weighted approaches to heat-health vulnerability in African contexts, enabling spatial non-stationarity analysis.
Cross-Climate Validation
Comparative analysis across subtropical highland and tropical savanna climates provides robust framework for African urban contexts.
Policy Applications
Spatially explicit vulnerability maps enable evidence-based targeting of climate adaptation interventions and health system preparedness.
Next Steps: Predictive Modeling Framework
  • Ensemble Machine Learning: Development of 24-72 hour heat-health risk forecasting
  • Causal Inference: Advanced explanatory modeling using SHAP/LIME interpretation
  • Scalability Assessment: Framework validation across additional African urban centers
  • Early Warning Systems: Integration with public health surveillance infrastructure
Timeline: 36-month PhD research program with quarterly milestones
Current Status: Vulnerability mapping phase completion (Month 18 of 36)
