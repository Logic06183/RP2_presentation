Research Portfolio & Evidence Base

Comprehensive research portfolio spanning 2002-2020 with 23 harmonized studies. Johannesburg leads with 15 studies (11,800 participants) while Abidjan contributes 8 studies (9,162 participants). Study categories include clinical trials, cohort studies, and community programs providing robust evidence for heat-health analysis.

Multi-Dimensional Climate Comparison

Four-year climate analysis (2020-2024) revealing distinct patterns: Johannesburg shows strong seasonal variation (5-25¬∞C) with dry heat extremes, while Abidjan maintains consistently high temperatures (22-32¬∞C) with humidity stress. Heat index categories demonstrate Abidjan's year-round "Caution" conditions versus Johannesburg's seasonal pattern.

Urban Heat Island Amplification

Critical urban heat island effects showing temperature anomalies from city centers. Johannesburg exhibits moderate UHI effect (+3.8¬∞C peak) while Abidjan shows severe amplification (+6.4¬∞C peak). These additional heat burdens significantly increase health risks for urban populations, particularly in densely populated areas.

Key Urban Heat Findings

  • Johannesburg: Moderate UHI effect concentrated in northern industrial areas
  • Abidjan: Severe coastal tropical UHI extending 10km from city center
  • 68% higher UHI intensity in Abidjan requiring different adaptation strategies
  • Both cities show asymmetric patterns influenced by prevailing winds and topography

Heat Stress & Health Risk Analysis

Heat index time series and temperature-humidity relationships demonstrate contrasting stress patterns. Abidjan experiences 550 "Caution" days (2020-2023) versus Johannesburg's 50 days. The temperature-humidity scatter plot reveals Abidjan's dangerous combination of high temperature AND high humidity, while Johannesburg shows dry heat conditions with lower humidity stress.

Spatial Heat Vulnerability Patterns

Geographically weighted principal component analysis for Johannesburg revealing spatial heterogeneity. PC1 (Environmental Exposure) shows elevated values in northern industrial corridors. PC2 (Health Vulnerability) identifies concentrated vulnerability in southern townships requiring targeted interventions.
Heat vulnerability index identifying top 10 most vulnerable wards encompassing 890,000 residents (18% of metropolitan population). This spatial targeting enables efficient resource allocation for maximum public health impact.

Key Findings & Policy Recommendations

Evidence-Based Interventions

  • Johannesburg: Implement dry heat early warning systems (>35¬∞C threshold)
  • Abidjan: Priority on humidity-heat monitoring and cooling infrastructure
  • Both Cities: Strengthen healthcare capacity during peak seasons
  • Investment: $290M combined annual economic impact requires urgent action
  • Future Risk: Plan for 2-3x increase in extreme heat days by 2050
